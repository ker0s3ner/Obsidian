## Introduction to Chemical Properties of Bases

Bases are substances that, in aqueous solution, are slippery to the touch, taste bitter, change the color of indicators (e.g., turn red litmus paper blue), react with acids to form salts, and promote certain chemical reactions. Their chemical properties largely stem from their ability to either donate hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$) or accept protons ($\text{H}^+$).

---

## Reactions with Acids (Neutralization)

One of the most fundamental properties of bases is their ability to react with acids in a process called **neutralization**. This reaction typically produces a salt and water.

> [!definition] Neutralization Reaction
> A chemical reaction in which an acid and a base react quantitatively with each other. In a reaction in water, neutralization results in there being no excess of hydrogen or hydroxide ions present in solution.

The general form of a neutralization reaction is:
$\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}$

### Examples

> [!example] Strong Acid-Strong Base Reaction
> When sodium hydroxide (a strong base) reacts with hydrochloric acid (a strong acid), it forms sodium chloride (a salt) and water.
> $$\text{NaOH(aq)} + \text{HCl(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O(l)}$$

> [!example] Divalent Base Reacting with an Acid
> When barium hydroxide reacts with sulfuric acid, it forms barium sulfate (which is insoluble and precipitates as a solid) and water.
> $$\text{Ba}(\text{OH})_2(\text{aq}) + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_4(\text{s}) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O(l)}$$
>
> In this specific case, the produced salt, barium sulfate, is a precipitate, meaning it comes out of solution as a solid.

> [!warning] Safety Precaution
> Always exercise caution when handling strong acids and bases. They are corrosive and can cause severe burns. Neutralization reactions, especially with concentrated solutions, can be highly exothermic (release heat).

## Reactions with Acidic Oxides

Bases can also react with **acidic oxides** (non-metal oxides that react with water to form an acid, or react with bases to form a salt and water). This reaction also forms a salt and water.

### Examples

> [!example] Calcium Hydroxide Reacting with Carbon Dioxide
> Calcium hydroxide (a base, often in solution as "limewater") reacts with carbon dioxide (an acidic oxide) to form calcium carbonate (an insoluble salt) and water. This is the reaction commonly observed when limewater turns cloudy in the presence of $\text{CO}_2$, indicating its presence.
> $$\text{Ca}(\text{OH})_2(\text{aq}) + \text{CO}_2(\text{g}) \rightarrow \text{CaCO}_3(\text{s}) + \text{H}_2\text{O(l)}$$

> [!example] Sodium Hydroxide Reacting with Sulfur Trioxide
> Sulfur trioxide ($\text{SO}_3$) is another acidic oxide, which reacts with a strong base like sodium hydroxide to produce sodium sulfate and water.
> $$2\text{NaOH(aq)} + \text{SO}_3(\text{g}) \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4(\text{aq}) + \text{H}_2\text{O(l)}$$

## Other Chemical Properties

### Reaction with Ammonium Salts

Strong bases react with ammonium salts to produce ammonia gas, water, and a salt. This reaction is often used as a test for ammonium ions ($\text{NH}_4^+$).

> [!example] Sodium Hydroxide and Ammonium Chloride
> $$\text{NaOH(aq)} + \text{NH}_4\text{Cl(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O(l)} + \text{NH}_3(\text{g})$$
> The ammonia gas ($\text{NH}_3$) produced has a pungent smell and turns damp red litmus paper blue.

### Acting as Ligands (Complex Formation)

Some transition metal ions react with strong bases to form precipitates of metal hydroxides. However, some metal hydroxides (like those of aluminum, zinc, and lead) are **amphoteric**, meaning they can redissolve in excess strong base to form soluble complex ions.

> [!example] Zinc Hydroxide and Excess Sodium Hydroxide
> 1. Precipitation: When sodium hydroxide is added to a zinc salt solution, zinc hydroxide precipitates.
> $$\text{Zn}^{2+}(\text{aq}) + 2\text{OH}^-(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{Zn}(\text{OH})_2(\text{s})$$
> 2. Complex formation: With excess sodium hydroxide, the zinc hydroxide redissolves to form a soluble complex ion.
> $$\text{Zn}(\text{OH})_2(\text{s}) + 2\text{OH}^-(\text{aq}) \rightarrow [\text{Zn}(\text{OH})_4]^{2-}(\text{aq})$$

## Practice Problems

1.  Write a balanced chemical equation for the reaction between potassium hydroxide ($\text{KOH}$) and nitric acid ($\text{HNO}_3$).
2.  Explain why limewater ($\text{Ca}(\text{OH})_2$) is used to test for carbon dioxide gas, including the relevant chemical equation.
3.  Magnesium hydroxide ($\text{Mg}(\text{OH})_2$) is a common antacid. Write the neutralization reaction it undergoes with stomach acid ($\text{HCl}$).
4.  Predict the products when sodium hydroxide reacts with sulfur dioxide ($\text{SO}_2$), an acidic oxide. Write the balanced equation.
5.  Describe what would be observed if a small amount of aqueous ammonia ($\text{NH}_4\text{OH}$) is added to a copper(II) sulfate solution, and then excess aqueous ammonia is added. (Hint: Copper(II) hydroxide is not amphoteric but forms a soluble complex with excess ammonia).
