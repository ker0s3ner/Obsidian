## Motivation

Classical calculus fails for random processes with rough paths. Stochastic calculus extends integration and differentiation to stochastic processes.

### Why Needed?

Brownian motion paths are:
- Continuous everywhere
- Differentiable nowhere
- Infinite variation on any interval

Standard Riemann-Stieltjes integral doesn't work!

---

## Brownian Motion (Wiener Process)

### Definition

**Standard Brownian motion** $W_t$ is stochastic process with:

1. $W_0 = 0$ (starts at zero)
2. Independent increments: $W_t - W_s$ independent of $\mathcal{F}_s$ for $t > s$
3. Gaussian increments: $W_t - W_s \sim N(0, t-s)$
4. Continuous paths: $t \mapsto W_t$ continuous almost surely

```graph3d
x: 0 .. 10
y: 0 .. 10
z: sum(sin(2^n*x + random())*2^(-n/2) + sin(2^n*y + random())*2^(-n/2), n, 1, 5)
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Brownian motion has fractal-like, extremely irregular paths.

### Properties

**Quadratic variation**:
$$[W]_t = \lim_{|\Pi| \to 0} \sum_{i}(W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i})^2 = t$$

This is key difference from smooth functions!

**Scaling property**: For $c > 0$:
$$\{c^{-1/2}W_{ct}\}_{t \geq 0} \stackrel{d}{=} \{W_t\}_{t \geq 0}$$

## Filtrations and Adapted Processes

### Filtration

**Filtration** $\{\mathcal{F}_t\}_{t \geq 0}$: Increasing family of sigma-algebras.

$\mathcal{F}_s \subseteq \mathcal{F}_t$ for $s < t$.

Represents "information available up to time $t$."

### Natural Filtration

For process $X_t$:
$$\mathcal{F}_t^X = \sigma(X_s : 0 \leq s \leq t)$$

Smallest filtration making $X$ adapted.

### Adapted Process

Process $X_t$ is **adapted** to $\mathcal{F}_t$ if $X_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable for all $t$.

Can only depend on information up to time $t$ (no "looking ahead").

## Simple Processes

### Definition

**Simple process** has form:
$$H_t = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} H_i \cdot \mathbb{1}_{(t_i, t_{i+1}]}(t)$$

where:
- $0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = T$ is partition
- $H_i$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t_i}$-measurable (bounded random variable)

Piecewise constant, adapted to filtration.

### Integral of Simple Process

For simple $H$:
$$\int_0^T H_t \, dW_t = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} H_i(W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i})$$

Well-defined since finite sum of random variables.

## Itô Isometry

### Theorem

For simple process $H$:
$$\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_0^T H_t \, dW_t\right)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T H_t^2 \, dt\right]$$

### Significance

- Relates stochastic integral to ordinary Lebesgue integral
- Shows $L^2$-isometry between integrand and integral
- Key tool for extending integral to general processes

### Proof Sketch

For simple $H = \sum H_i \mathbb{1}_{(t_i,t_{i+1}]}$:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\sum H_i \Delta W_i\right)^2\right] &= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum H_i^2 (\Delta W_i)^2 + 2\sum_{i<j} H_i H_j \Delta W_i \Delta W_j\right] \\ &= \sum \mathbb{E}[H_i^2]\mathbb{E}[(\Delta W_i)^2] + 0 \\ &= \sum \mathbb{E}[H_i^2](t_{i+1}-t_i) \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T H_t^2 \, dt\right] \end{aligned}$$

Cross terms vanish by independence!

## Itô Integral Construction

### Extension to General Processes

For adapted process $H$ with $\mathbb{E}\int_0^T H_t^2 \, dt < \infty$:

1. Approximate $H$ by simple processes $H^{(n)}$
2. Define $\int_0^T H_t \, dW_t = L^2\text{-}\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^T H_t^{(n)} \, dW_t$
3. Limit exists and is unique by Itô isometry

### Notation

$$\int_0^t H_s \, dW_s \quad \text{or} \quad \int_0^t H \, dW$$

Result is random variable (depends on $\omega$).

### Properties

1. **Linearity**: $\int (aH + bK) \, dW = a\int H \, dW + b\int K \, dW$
2. **Martingale**: $M_t = \int_0^t H_s \, dW_s$ is martingale
3. **Zero mean**: $\mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^t H_s \, dW_s\right] = 0$
4. **Itô isometry**: $\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_0^t H \, dW\right)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\int_0^t H^2 \, ds$

## Examples

### Example 1: Constant Integrand

$$\int_0^t 1 \, dW_s = W_t - W_0 = W_t$$

### Example 2: Deterministic Function

$$\int_0^t s \, dW_s \sim N\left(0, \int_0^t s^2 \, ds\right) = N\left(0, \frac{t^3}{3}\right)$$

Gaussian with variance $\frac{t^3}{3}$.

### Example 3: Brownian Motion Itself

$$\int_0^t W_s \, dW_s = \frac{1}{2}W_t^2 - \frac{t}{2}$$

Note: NOT $\frac{1}{2}W_t^2$! Extra term $-\frac{t}{2}$ comes from Itô's formula.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Treating $dW_t$ Like Ordinary Differential
> $(dW_t)^2 = dt$, not zero! This is fundamental difference from classical calculus.

> [!warning] Forgetting Adaptedness
> Integrand must be adapted (cannot depend on future). Non-anticipating!

> [!warning] Assuming Chain Rule Works
> Classical chain rule fails. Must use Itô's formula instead.
